]]> By: earwicker http://adventuresinsoftware.com/blog/?p=49#comment-229 earwicker Fri, 17 Nov 2006 05:53:37 +0000 http://adventuresinsoftware.com/blog/?p=49#comment-229 Another non-Jim-related question: does your decentralized scenario have an implicit assumption of benevolence on the part of all participants? Particularly when we're talking about a geographically diverse group of large cardinality, what is to stop a malicious member from randomly revoking the membership of others? Granted, this is at worst an inconvenience, as those memberships can be reinstated by any other member . . . but if you've ever spent time in online communities with open membership policies, you know that a single jerk can play havoc with the well-behaved members of the community. And you end up, once again, with one or more trusted people in a centralized position of authority (can you say "root?") in order to overcome this nuisance and maintain order. I'll grant that this may be slightly better, in that the centralized authority is only required in the presence of malicious users: not so likely in small workplace environments, but with a likelihood approaching certainty as the groupsize (and/or anonymity level) grows.
